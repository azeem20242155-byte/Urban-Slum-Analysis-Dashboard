## Central Question

Does urban growth lead to improved housing conditions — or can housing 
pressure increase even while slum percentages appear to decline?

This dashboard shows that both can be true at the same time. The global 
average slum share fell from 32.7% in 2000 to 23.5% in 2022, yet the 
estimated number of people living in slums nearly doubled from 457 million 
to 894 million over the same period. Percentages improved while the human 
burden increased. That paradox is the central finding this dashboard is 
built around.

## Key Findings

- Global slum share fell from **32.7% (2000) to 23.5% (2022)**, but 
  estimated slum population rose from **457 million to 894 million**
- **105 out of 195 countries** urbanised while reducing slum share; 
  **33 countries** urbanised while slum conditions worsened
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** carries the highest concentration of severe slum 
  conditions, visible clearly in the 2022 choropleth map

The analysis shows that urban population growth does not produce the same outcomes across countries.  

Some countries appear to manage urbanisation effectively, with declining slum conditions, while others experience increasing housing pressure and expanding slum populations.  

This highlights the importance of urban planning, infrastructure development and housing policy in shaping development outcomes.

  ## Objectives

The main objectives of this project are:

- To analyse trends in urban population growth across countries  
- To examine changes in slum population and slum share  
- To identify patterns between urbanisation and housing conditions  
- To classify countries into risk categories based on observed trends  
- To present insights through an interactive dashboard  


## Dashboard Structure

The dashboard consists of four main sections:

**Global Overview**  
Provides a high-level summary of urban population trends and slum indicators.

**Urban vs Slums Analysis**  
Explores the relationship between urban growth and changes in slum population.

**Country Explorer**  
Allows detailed analysis of individual countries.

**Risk Classification**  
Groups countries into categories based on:
- Urban population growth  
- Change in slum share  
- Change in estimated slum population  

The categories include:
- Improving urbanisation  
- Growth with pressure  
- Housing deterioration  
- Low growth / mixed outcome  

Interactive features such as filters, sorting options and hover information are included to improve usability.


## Methodology

The project follows a structured data pipeline approach:

1. Data collection  
   Data was obtained from the World Bank World Development Indicators.

2. Data cleaning and preparation  
   Missing values were handled and datasets were aligned by country and time period.

3. Feature engineering  
   Key variables were created, including:
   - Total urban population growth (%)  
   - Change in slum share (percentage points)  
   - Change in slum population  

4. Risk classification  
   Countries were grouped based on the direction of change in urban growth and slum indicators.  
   A simple scoring approach was also applied to support comparison.

The classification is intended to support interpretation rather than to act as a predictive model.

## Data Sources

Source: UN-Habitat / World Bank WDI

2. Urban population  
Source: United Nations World Urbanization Prospects / World Bank WDI

  ## Derived Metrics

Neither dataset alone provides the full picture. The following metrics 
were engineered during data preparation:


- Estimated Slum Population - This is the key metric that makes the paradox visible.
     Estimated Slum Population = (Slum % / 100) × Urban Population
- `urban_growth_total` — percentage growth in urban population
- `slum_change_total` — change in slum share in percentage points
- `slum_population_change_total` — change in absolute estimated slum population

These three fields drive the classification logic across the 
Urbanisation vs Slums and Risk Classification pages.

## Key Technical Decisions

**Coverage filtering**  
Years 2009 and 2019 contained only 1 and 3 country records respectively 
in the slum dataset, causing false spikes in global trend averages. 
A threshold filter was applied to exclude years where country coverage 
fell below 70% of the maximum, removing these artefacts.

**Aggregate code removal**  
The raw dataset includes 48 World Bank regional aggregate rows 
(e.g. WLD, HIC, SSA, ARB). These were removed by filtering on 
`country_code` using a predefined set, not by country name, to ensure 
no aggregate rows contaminate any grouped calculation.

**Slider edge issue**  
18 countries (including China, Japan, Malaysia) appear only once in 
the merged dataset. A conditional check prevents Streamlit's range 
slider from crashing when year_min == year_max.

**Outlier capping for visualisation**  
Extreme urban growth outliers were capped at 300% for scatterplot scaling 
to preserve interpretability. This affects visual display only and does not alter underlying data.
